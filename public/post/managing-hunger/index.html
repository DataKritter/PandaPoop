Feeding "hungry" animals

2 Sep 2018
3 min read
  • How often does your animal eat in the wild?
  • When does your animal eat in the wild?
  • Does your animal normally consume meals? or continuously browse/graze?
  • When should I change the diet?

People often want to increase an animal’s diet because it is “acting hungry” or it is “cleaning up” its diet. The problem with this philosophy is that an animal’s appetite at a given moment and its overall energy balance are rarely correlated. An obese animal can feel hungry and an emaciated one can feel satiated. The answer is not necessarily to change the diet (unless the animal is not in ideal body condition), but to change the feeding plan.

How often does your animal eat in the wild? 

Domestic cats eat dozens of small meals a day. Lions and wolves gorge and then fast for days. Hummingbirds eat nearly constantly. Nocturnal animals may only experience hunger when it is dark. If you only feed your hummingbird 4 times a day, it likely is experiencing hunger between meals, but a tiger fed 4 times a day may never quite feel satiated by these tiny meals and always want more.

When does your animal eat in the wild? 

Nocturnal animals may be unlikely to eat during bright daylight hours, and diurnal animals may not budge to eat food at night.

Does your animal normally consume meals? or continuously browse/graze? 

Animals that graze, browse or hunt multiple small prey usually spend many (most) hours a day gathering enough food. In human care, they might be able to eat their entire daily intake in under an hour, leaving them 5-10 hours to fill with…begging? The key to these species is to keep them busy using slow feeders and other challenging feeding plans. Feeding is their job, and it shouldn’t be easy. Keep them moving, manipulating, and working to spend 8 hours to get enough to eat.
Consumers of larger prey or foods might have long gaps between meals, but typically consume large meals. In human care, often we want to be able to feed small meals throughout the day as part of training or husbandry sessions. This is often in conflict with their natural feeding patterns. One way to work with this is to feed much smaller portions as part of sessions, and offer large meals at other times. One option is a gorge day followed by a fast day, with training happening on the other 5 days of the week. Another alternative is multiple small training sessions totalling around 20% of the diet, followed by a feed with the remaining 80%.

When should I change the diet? 

When the animal’s body condition drops above or below its target range. You can find body condition scales for a variety of species at the AZA Nutrition Advisory Group’s Body Condition Scoring Resource Center.

You might also change the form. You can add water to the diet to make an animal “feel fuller”. You can add binder to some carnivore diets (i.e., solkafloc) to make them last longer (and improve stool quality). You can mix food into unflavored gelatin to make it more of a challenge, or put it into puzzle feeders and enrichment devices.

By carefully evaluating symptoms of hunger in your animals, you can develop a feeding plan that fits their natural foraging pattern, your husbandry needs, and the animals’ nutritional needs.
